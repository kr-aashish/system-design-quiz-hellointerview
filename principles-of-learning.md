# Principles of Learning — Master Reference

> **Purpose:** This file is the single source of truth for *how* to learn. It will guide the design of future system design (SD2) study sessions, interactive quizzes, and review cycles. Every quiz, flashcard, or practice exercise built for the SD2 interview prep should be checked against the principles below to make sure it actively uses them rather than slipping into passive study.
>
> **Overarching theme:** *Encoding Mastered* — getting information into the brain so it actually sticks.

---

## 0. Mindset Foundations (the meta-frame)

- "Good vs. bad memory" is a myth — there is only a **trained** memory and an **untrained** memory.
- Every brain is fundamentally the same hardware; the difference is training.
- Tied to **Neuroplasticity + Grit** — the brain physically rewires with effort over time.
- Implication for SD2: progress is not a question of talent; it is a question of how systematically the principles below are applied.

---

## 1. Foundational Principles

### 1.1 Attention
- At the most basic level, **we remember what we pay attention to**.
- Mastering attention ≈ getting "super memory."

### 1.2 Understanding
- New info must be **meaningful in light of what's already in your mind**.
- Always connect new material to existing knowledge — never memorize in isolation.

### 1.3 Focus
- **Distraction** = like fencing off your octopus's arms — you lose grip on what you're learning.
- **Multitasking is actually task-switching** — it tires/stresses the "octopus" (your attention). Avoid it.

### 1.4 Diffused Mode
- Insight and consolidation happen during **breaks**, not just focused work.
- Relaxed focus → creativity.
- Alternate **focused mode** (deliberate study) with **diffused mode** (walks, showers, rest).

### 1.5 Struggle = Better Learning (Desirable Difficulty)
- Struggling during learning produces stronger retention.
- Harder material → better learning, **as long as it's just at the edge of your ability**.

## 2. Memory Tricks — The Practical Toolkit

### 2.1 Feel It → Emotion
- Learn with **joy, excitement, curiosity, fascination** (e.g. good YouTube explainers).
- Emotional encoding makes memories far stickier.

### 2.2 Visualization
- **What:** Convert info into pictures, funny stories (made of pictures), and locations (memory palace style).
- **Why it works:** Information combined / associated / connected with spatial, visual, or emotional memory acts like a magnet for recall.
- The brain has exceptional memory for **pictures and spatial locations** (evolutionarily ancient systems).
- Use **whenever feasible** — even for abstract system design concepts (draw the architecture, picture the data flowing through queues, etc.).

### 2.3 Metaphor & Mnemonics
- They piggyback on **existing neural connections**, so new material hooks into something already learned.
- Use whenever possible.

### 2.4 Pictures over Text
- Pictures >> text for the brain.
- Use **keywords** anchored to images, with associated sentences/paragraphs.

### 2.5 Music
- Music can aid encoding/recall.

### 2.6 Multiple Mediums (use several input channels)
- **See** it.
- **Listen** to it.
- **Record** it — make audio "zest" of what you've learned (audible memory can be stronger than visual).
- **Mere exposure / frequency** — repeated encounters build familiarity.
- **Source matters** — use credible sources.

### 2.7 Initial Practice (right after learning)
- Practicing immediately after first learning is hugely efficient — locks the trace in fast.
- "Right after learning!!" → put it into Anki, then practice.

### 2.8 Serial Position Effect
- We remember the **beginning and end** of a session best; the **middle is weakest**.
- Put the most important material at the **start and end**.
- **Learn opposites together** to leverage contrast.

### 2.9 Feynman Technique
- Teach what you just learned — even to an imaginary student.
- Forces **active recall** and exposes gaps in your understanding.

### 2.10 One at a Time
- Learn **step by step, sequentially** — one concept at a time.
- Don't try to swallow everything at once.

### 2.11 Handwriting > Typing
- Don't type — **write by hand**, or better, recall and *then* write.
- Handwriting forces deeper processing.

### 2.12 Structured Manner
- Before starting: read the **summary / overview first**.
- Why: builds a **knowledge skeleton** so new details have somewhere to attach.
- Same principle behind **mind maps**.

### 2.13 Before Starting — Research Phase
- Decide exactly *what* you want to learn and *why*.
- **Deconstruct the skill** — practice the most important sub-skills first.
- Identify the most important sub-skills and gather resources (but don't let research become procrastination).
- **Remove barriers to practice**: turn off TV, block the internet, close the door, silence the phone.
- **Pre-commit to ~20 hours of focused practice** before judging progress.

### 2.14 Note-Taking
- Don't take passive notes — it's the most passive and least effective method.
- If you must, use **Cornell-style notes** with toggle questions for active recall.

### 2.15 Read Faster
- Use a **visual pacer** (finger / pen).
- Read **groups of words / phrases** at once — take meaning from chunks, not single words.
- See only **main keywords** to grasp the meaning of an entire sentence.
- Use **Spreed** (or similar tools).
- For books: prefer **summarized versions / YouTube summaries**, then read cover-to-cover only what *resonates*.

## 3. Lifestyle (the body supports the brain)

- **Exercise:** heart-rate-up sessions, **~4× per week, ~30 min** — promotes neuron growth.
- **Hydration:** brain is **~80% water**; dehydration directly tanks cognition.
- **Sugar:** harmful to learning; counter with **Omega-3s**.
- **Sleep** (the most important):
  - Lack of sleep destroys neurons and prunes millions of synaptic connections.
  - **Evening routine: review (don't learn new) before sleep** — grows dendritic spines better.
- **Posture:**
  - Sit/stand straight.
  - Why: poor posture crimps blood supply to the brain.
  - Don't stay seated long — walk regularly; rest the eyes frequently.

---

## 4. Active Recall — The Core Engine

- **What to do:** Look away from the material and try to recall key ideas/concepts.
- **How:**
  - Use **keywords**.
  - Generate and answer **questions**.
- **Avoid passive methods:** re-reading, highlighting, summarizing — they *feel* productive but are weak.
- **Become an active-recall machine:**
  - Recall in a **completely randomized fashion** (not just immediately after coding/studying a topic).
  - Do **cover-to-cover active recall**.
- Use **Anki** for isolated facts.
- Maintain **Master Sheets / Notion** for structured review.

---

## 5. Spaced Repetition

- Reviewing right after learning + spaced reviews can improve retention by **~80%**.
- **Same-day review:** after every second topic, recall the previous one.
- **Master Sheets / Notion review cycle:** review at **1 day → 1 week → 1 month** intervals.
- Trust your memory — you don't need to cram everything at the last moment.

### 5.1 Interleaving (mixing it up)
- Mix subjects/topics instead of blocking one for hours.
- **Practice in pieces**, not all at once.
- Why it works: the brain remembers what it leaves unfinished — interleaving keeps loops open.
- **How:** take breaks, switch subjects (e.g. switch sub-topic at a break).
- **Don't:** drill the same kind of thing all at once (no all-similar-at-once blocks).
- This is the principle behind **Anki**.

### 5.2 Anki
- Use Anki for **newly learned, very isolated facts** — things that don't fit a larger structure.

---

## 6. Practice

- **Practice makes permanent** — and, if done right, *perfect* (literally rewires neurons).
- **Quality > quantity:** do less, but make it **fully hands-on**.
- **Hard Start / Dual-Core Processing** (for tests/practice):
  - Start with the **hardest problem**, work briefly, then switch to easier ones.
  - Engages both focused and diffused modes simultaneously on the hard problem.
- **Frequency / How:**
  - Practice **in pieces** — distributed, not crammed.
  - Spread sessions over **two days instead of one** when possible.
  - All-at-once cramming is crap.
  - Build in **repetition via review and re-practice**.

---

## 7. The Core Loop (if you remember nothing else)

1. **Pay attention + understand** (connect to existing knowledge).
2. **Encode richly** — emotion, visualization, mnemonics, multiple senses, handwriting.
3. **Struggle a little** — desirable difficulty.
4. **Active recall** instead of re-reading.
5. **Space the repetitions** — same day → 1 day → 1 week → 1 month.
6. **Interleave** topics; practice in pieces.
7. **Sleep, exercise, hydrate, posture** — the brain is biological hardware.
8. **Teach it** (Feynman) to seal it in.

## 9. Application Rubric for SD2 Quiz Generation

When future system design content is provided and quizzes are generated from it, every quiz should be checked against this rubric:

1. **Attention/Focus:** Does the quiz force one concept at a time, with no distractors?
2. **Understanding:** Does it require connecting new SD concepts to known ones (e.g. "this is like X you already know")?
3. **Active Recall:** Are questions phrased to force recall (no re-reading; cover-the-answer style)?
4. **Desirable Difficulty:** Is the question slightly harder than passive recall — e.g. "design", "trade-off", "what breaks if…"?
5. **Visualization:** Does it ask the learner to draw / picture / sketch architecture rather than just list components?
6. **Metaphor/Mnemonic:** Where possible, are concepts wrapped in a mnemonic or metaphor?
7. **Serial Position:** Is the most important content at the **start and end** of the session?
8. **Opposites Together:** Are contrasting concepts presented as a pair (e.g. SQL vs. NoSQL, push vs. pull, sync vs. async)?
9. **Feynman Trigger:** Is there at least one "explain it to a beginner" prompt?
10. **Interleaving:** Across a study set, are topics mixed rather than blocked?
11. **Spacing:** Are the same items scheduled for review at 1 day → 1 week → 1 month?
12. **Hard Start:** Does the practice/test set begin with a hard, open-ended design problem?
13. **Hands-on:** Does the quiz require *producing* something (a design, a calculation, a trade-off table) rather than just selecting an answer?
14. **Handwriting/Drawing prompt:** Where appropriate, does the quiz say "sketch this on paper before answering"?
15. **Anki-suitable isolated facts:** Are atomic facts (e.g. "what is the read latency of Cassandra typically?") split out for Anki?
16. **Lifestyle hooks:** Long sessions should include break/walk reminders.
17. **Hard distractors (discrimination quality):** For every MCQ, the three wrong options must be length-matched to the correct option (within ~15% character count), share its vocabulary register and clause structure, and each represent a *real misconception* a moderately-prepared SDE2 candidate would seriously consider — not strawmen, off-topic asides, or absurd claims. A learner must not be able to pick the correct answer by spotting which option is the longest, the most hedged, or the only one using precise terminology. The correct answer's position (`correctIndex`) must vary across the file so position itself is never a tell. Full authoring procedure and acid tests are in `quiz-generation-methodology.md` Section 0.6.
